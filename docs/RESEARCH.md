Reading stamped codes on steel billets: a complete POC blueprint
The most reliable path to 90%+ accuracy on steel billet stamp recognition combines a two-stage detection-recognition pipeline with domain-specific fine-tuning — not a single off-the-shelf model. Recent academic work on exactly this problem (billet number recognition using DBNet + SVTR via PaddleOCR) confirms the approach works in production steel mills, achieving 91–94% weekly recognition rates on spray-marked characters. Commercial systems from Sysfo and Cognex already achieve near-100% accuracy using controlled lighting and AI-based OCR, Sysfo proving the ceiling is high. sysfo The key insight: no foundation model reads dot-matrix stamps on oxidized steel well out of the box, but fine-tuned models on even 500–2000 labeled images can reach production-grade accuracy.

The landscape of approaches, from quick wins to production systems
Three fundamentally different architectures can tackle this problem, each with distinct tradeoffs in accuracy, cost, and time-to-deploy.
Vision Language Models (VLMs) like GPT-4o, Gemini 2.5 Flash, and Qwen3-VL offer the fastest path to a working prototype. Feed a billet image with a carefully engineered prompt, and they return the stamped code. On general OCR benchmarks, Gemini 2.5 Pro scores 88.03 on OmniDocBench (highest among all VLMs), while Qwen2.5-VL-72B scores 888/1000 on OCRBench (best open-source). Without fine-tuning, expect 70–85% accuracy on billet stamps — useful for validation but insufficient for production. The critical advantage of open-source VLMs like Qwen3-VL is fine-tunability: practitioners report pushing accuracy from baseline 93% to 99%+ after domain-specific LoRA training on just hundreds of examples. Arsturn
Traditional and deep learning OCR pipelines (PaddleOCR, TrOCR, PARSeq) are purpose-built for text recognition and run orders of magnitude faster than VLMs. PaddleOCR PP-OCRv5 delivers the best out-of-box accuracy among open-source OCR engines, with 13-point accuracy gains over its predecessor GitHub and built-in angle classification for skewed text. TrOCR achieves 98.73% exact match on container ISO codes after fine-tuning. ResearchGate PARSeq reaches 96.0% accuracy on scene text benchmarks with real training data. ResearchGate These models need a separate text detection stage but can process images in 50–140ms versus 1–8 seconds for VLMs.
Hybrid detection + recognition pipelines represent the proven approach in steel-specific literature. The most directly relevant paper — "Billet Number Recognition Based on Test-Time Adaptation" (Wei & Zhou, February 2025) — uses DBNet for text detection + SVTR for recognition within PaddleOCR, enhanced with test-time adaptation to handle domain shift between training and real-world oxidized surfaces. arXiv A parallel production system running PaddleOCR on steel plate slab markings has operated stably for over a year with a minimum weekly positive rate of 91.2%. Why preprocessing makes or breaks billet stamp recognition
The single most impactful software intervention is CLAHE (Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalization), which adaptively enhances local contrast across the image — essential when different areas of an oxidized billet surface have wildly different reflectance. Applied on the L channel of LAB color space with a clipLimit of 2.0–4.0 and tileGridSize of (8,8), CLAHE can transform invisible stamps into clearly readable text. Research consistently identifies it as the critical preprocessing step for low-contrast industrial text.
The single most impactful hardware intervention is multi-directional illumination. Springer Sysfo's production OCR stations use 4 directional LED lights to capture shadows from different angles, then fuse the images to maximize embossed character contrast. This technique alone enables "almost 100% recognition reliability" in their steel mill installations. Sysfosysfo For the POC, even a simple low-angle ring light dramatically improves stamp visibility by casting shadows in the stamped indentations.
The recommended preprocessing pipeline, in order:

ROI extraction — Detect the billet end face boundary (circle/ellipse detection or a trained detector) and crop to the text region, eliminating background noise from surrounding areas and yellow paint markings
Perspective correction — Compute homography from the billet face plane to a fronto-parallel view using cv2.warpPerspective, correcting for camera angle variation OpenCV
Bilateral filtering (d=9, sigmaColor=75, sigmaSpace=75) — Smooths rust and scale texture while preserving the sharp edges of stamped characters, outperforming Gaussian blur for this application
CLAHE enhancement on grayscale — The critical contrast boost step
Optional edge sharpening via unsharp masking for very faint stamps

Deep learning OCR models generally perform better on CLAHE-enhanced grayscale images than on binarized images. Skip adaptive thresholding when feeding into neural networks — it destroys gradient information that models can leverage.

The optimal two-stage architecture for 90%+ accuracy
Based on all evidence, the recommended architecture is a two-stage detection-recognition pipeline with domain-specific fine-tuning, augmented by structured post-processing that exploits billet number encoding rules.
Stage 1 — Text detection uses DBNet++ (Differentiable Binarization), the same detector proven in the billet domain by Wei & Zhou (2025). DBNet++ integrates binarization as a differentiable module during training, IntuitionLabs achieving 93.52% end-to-end recall and real-time inference speed. It runs as PaddleOCR's default detection backbone. Pixno For the POC, fine-tune DBNet++ on 500+ annotated billet images with bounding boxes around text regions.
Stage 2 — Text recognition uses SVTR (Scene Visual Transformer) or PARSeq to read the cropped text regions. SVTR, used in the billet recognition paper, is optimized for CTC-based recognition with a visual transformer backbone. ReadthedocsarXiv PARSeq achieves 96.0% on standard scene text benchmarks and is Pareto-optimal for accuracy versus efficiency. Ecva Both are available in PaddleOCR and docTR respectively, GitHubIntuitionLabs and both fine-tune well on custom datasets.
Stage 3 — Structured post-processing applies billet number encoding rules as prior knowledge: company identifiers must be letters, production dates must be valid, heat numbers follow known digit patterns. The test-time adaptation paper showed that injecting these constraints into CTC decoding dramatically improves word-level accuracy by correcting character-level confusions (0/O, 1/I, 8/B) using format context. arXiv

Building the training dataset from scratch
The training data strategy combines three sources in a specific sequence to maximize accuracy while minimizing annotation cost.
Synthetic pre-training (10K–100K images) bootstraps the model before any real data is available. Use TRDG (TextRecognitionDataGenerator) or SynthTIGER to render dot-matrix style text using industrial stencil and OCR-A/B fonts onto photographed or procedurally generated oxidized metal textures. Docsaid The "Synthetic Recipe for OCR" research found that font diversity is critical — models trained with 60+ fonts significantly outperform single-font models. Jhu Apply realistic degradations: rust overlay textures, specular reflections, partial occlusion, varying illumination. Generate text following actual billet number formats (6-digit heat numbers, strand identifiers, date codes).
Real image collection and annotation (500–2000 images) provides the domain-specific signal that synthetic data cannot fully capture. Photograph billet end faces across the full range of conditions: fresh stamps, oxidized, wet, scaled, various lighting. Active learning accelerates this phase: train an initial model on synthetic data, deploy it on real images, and use uncertainty sampling to select the most informative images for human labeling. This approach typically achieves 40–70% reduction in annotation cost compared to random sampling. Cedextraining
Data augmentation (applied to all images) multiplies the effective dataset size. Domain-specific augmentations include: erosion and dilation (simulating thicker/thinner stamp impressions), MediumTowards Data Science random brightness/contrast adjustment (simulating varying plant lighting), perspective distortion (simulating camera angle variation), and partial occlusion masks (simulating scale or debris covering characters). From POC to production: a phased implementation plan
Phase 1 — Baseline validation (1–2 weeks). Use Gemini 2.5 Flash API (cheapest at ~$0.001/image) or GPT-4o with carefully engineered prompts to process 50–100 real billet images. The prompt should specify: "Read the alphanumeric heat number stamped on this steel billet end face. Characters are dot-matrix style, uppercase A-Z and digits 0-9. The heat number is typically 6 digits. Return only the characters you can read with high confidence." Set temperature to 0.0 for deterministic output. TRM This establishes a baseline accuracy figure (expect 70–85%) and identifies the hardest failure modes. Total cost: under $10 in API fees.
Phase 2 — Preprocessing pipeline + PaddleOCR (2–4 weeks). Implement the CLAHE-centered preprocessing pipeline in OpenCV. Deploy PaddleOCR PP-OCRv5 with DBNet++ detection and SVTR recognition. Test on the same 100 images to measure preprocessing impact. Begin collecting and annotating real billet images — target 500 initially. Generate 10K–50K synthetic images using TRDG with metal textures. Fine-tune detection and recognition models on the combined dataset. Expected accuracy improvement: +10–15 percentage points over Phase 1. Total infrastructure cost: a single GPU machine.
Phase 3 — Fine-tuned VLM for difficult cases (2–4 weeks). Fine-tune Qwen3-VL-2B or GOT-OCR-2.0 using LoRA on 500–2000 labeled billet images. This model handles the cases where the preprocessing + PaddleOCR pipeline fails — severely degraded stamps, unusual angles, partial occlusions. Deploy as a fallback: if PaddleOCR confidence falls below threshold (0.85), route to the fine-tuned VLM. The ensemble of fast pipeline + powerful VLM fallback targets 95%+ overall accuracy. Infrastructure: one NVIDIA A100 or H100 for training (cloud rental: ~$2–3/hour), one consumer GPU (RTX 4090) for inference.
Phase 4 — Edge deployment and production hardening (4–8 weeks). Optimize the pipeline for NVIDIA Jetson Orin NX ($700–900) using TensorRT. Implement the post-processing rules engine (billet number format validation, database cross-reference against production schedule). Build the active learning loop: log all inferences with confidence scores, route uncertain cases to human reviewers, retrain monthly on validated production data. Conclusion
The steel billet stamp reading problem sits at an interesting intersection: it is technically solved by commercial systems (Sysfo achieves near-100% with controlled lighting), Sysfosysfo yet remains challenging for general-purpose AI models without domain adaptation. The fastest path to a working POC is a two-stage PaddleOCR pipeline (DBNet++ detection + SVTR recognition) with CLAHE preprocessing, fine-tuned on a mix of synthetic and real billet images. This approach is proven by the February 2025 billet recognition paper, runs in under 200ms, deploys offline on edge hardware, and achieves 90%+ accuracy with modest training data.
The most underappreciated insight from this research is that hardware (lighting) matters as much as software (models). Multi-directional LED illumination transforms the problem from "impossible low-contrast stamps" to "clearly visible embossed text" Springer — potentially adding more accuracy than any model architecture choice. Springersysfo For the POC, start with controlled lighting before investing heavily in model engineering.
For organizations wanting the highest possible accuracy with minimal engineering effort, the fine-tuned VLM path (Qwen3-VL-2B with LoRA, or GOT-OCR-2.0) offers a compelling alternative that eliminates the need to build separate detection and recognition stages. The two approaches are not mutually exclusive — the optimal production system likely uses the fast PaddleOCR pipeline for 90% of images and routes difficult cases to a fine-tuned VLM for verification, achieving 98%+ overall accuracy while maintaining sub-second latency.
